Clio Research-Based Project

Project Overview
- Clio is a modern writing environment focused on academic integrity. It combines a rich editor, behavioral analytics, and high-fidelity exports to help verify authorship and preserve document quality.
- Core capabilities:
  - Rich writing with TipTap, headings, lists, text styling, and inline images.
  - Behavioral signals: typing speed (WPM) and paste detection as lightweight integrity indicators.
  - Export pipeline: DOCX generation on the client and server conversion to PDF for a print-quality output.
  - Optional Spotify integration to support focus without leaving the writing context.

Problem Statement
- Academic institutions need ways to verify that submitted work is authored by the claimed student, especially in a world with abundant copy-paste and AI assistance.
- Traditional tools (e.g., MS Word, Obsidian) excel at editing but do not provide integrated authorship integrity signals during composition.
- Clio addresses the gap by providing integrity-aware writing workflows while preserving document fidelity and usability.

Research Questions
- RQ1: How reliably do simple behavioral signals (WPM trends, paste events) indicate authorship integrity during essay writing?
- RQ2: How accurate is Clio’s PDF export fidelity compared to the authored content (headings, lists, images, styles)?
- RQ3: How does usability and task efficiency in Clio compare to Obsidian and MS Word for typical student writing tasks?
- RQ4: Does support tooling (e.g., integrated music playback) improve focus and perceived writing quality?
- RQ5: What are the privacy and ethical boundaries for collecting and using behavioral writing signals?

Hypotheses
- H1: Paste detection correlates with integrity risks; frequent paste events lower a projected integrity score.
- H2: The DOCX→PDF pipeline preserves structure and images at comparable fidelity to MS Word’s built-in PDF export for common academic documents.
- H3: For focused essay writing, Clio’s streamlined UI yields similar or better usability ratings than Obsidian and MS Word.
- H4: Ambient support features (music) increase focus ratings without harming productivity.

Methodology
- Technical evaluation:
  - Build a standardized set of essays with headings, lists, and images; export to DOCX and PDF; check structural preservation.
  - Fidelity checks: count preserved headings, list items, images, and compare image dimensions; spot-check colors and spacing.
- Behavioral signals study:
  - Within-subject experiment where participants write short essays in Clio; log WPM and paste events.
  - Ground truth: ask participants to intentionally paste text at certain times to measure detection precision/recall.
- Usability comparison (Clio vs Obsidian vs MS Word):
  - Tasks: write a two-page essay with headings, one list, and three images; export to PDF.
  - Metrics: task completion time, error rate, SUS scores, NASA‑TLX workload, perceived focus and satisfaction.
- Spotify integration usability:
  - Measure time to connect and playback control success rate; survey whether it helps focus.

Comparative Analysis
- Obsidian:
  - Strengths: knowledge management, Markdown, graph view, plugin ecosystem.
  - Limitations vs Clio: no built-in authorship integrity analytics; export fidelity depends on Markdown/CSS; image handling differs.
  - Clio advantage: authorship signals during writing, integrated rich‑text editing rather than Markdown, streamlined export flow designed for academic essays.
- MS Word:
  - Strengths: mature WYSIWYG, track changes, excellent local PDF export, grammar tools.
  - Limitations vs Clio: lacks integrated authorship integrity indicators; paste is allowed and not surfaced as an integrity risk.
  - Clio advantage: real‑time integrity signals; comparable document fidelity for typical academic formatting via DOCX→PDF pipeline; lightweight web deployment.

Metrics
- Integrity score:
  - Components: paste events per 1,000 words; WPM anomaly thresholds; rapid bursts vs steady typing.
  - Report precision/recall for paste detection on labeled events.
- Export fidelity score:
  - Structural preservation rate: headings, lists, paragraph count match.
  - Image preservation rate: count, resolution, layout approximate match.
  - Style fidelity: bold/italic presence, heading levels preserved.
- Usability and focus:
  - SUS (System Usability Scale) target ≥ 70.
  - NASA‑TLX workload lower is better; measure mental demand and frustration.
  - Task time and error rate.

Ethics and Privacy
- Informed consent: clearly state what behavioral signals are collected (WPM, paste events) and why.
- Minimal data: avoid recording sensitive content or keystrokes beyond aggregate metrics; no passwords or secrets.
- Storage and retention: keep only anonymized aggregates for analysis; allow opt‑out.

Technical Architecture
- Frontend:
  - Rich editor: `frontend/components/editor/RichEditor.tsx` using TipTap with Image, TextStyle, TextAlign.
  - Writer page: `frontend/app/editor/page.tsx` handles exports, integrity signals, and UI.
  - Spotify integration: `frontend/components/ui/spotify-card.tsx` with PKCE/implicit auth and Web Playback SDK.
- Export pipeline:
  - DOCX builder: `docx` with `Paragraph`, `TextRun`, `ImageRun`; reads editor HTML, embeds images from data URLs.
  - PDF conversion route: `frontend/app/api/convert-docx-to-pdf/route.ts` (server) uses `mammoth` to HTML and `puppeteer` to render PDF.
- Auth examples:
  - Spotify token exchange: `frontend/app/api/spotify/token/route.ts`.
  - Callback page: `frontend/app/spotify/callback/page.tsx`.

Limitations
- Integrity signals are heuristic and cannot prove authorship; they raise flags for review.
- PDF fidelity depends on HTML rendering; complex layouts may need tuning.
- Web Crypto availability affects PKCE flow; fallback used where necessary.
- No advanced stylometry or ML in current scope.

Competitive Landscape and Differentiation
- Focused authorship integrity vs general‑purpose editing:
  - Clio provides real‑time paste detection, WPM trends, and integrity indicators during writing. General tools (Notion, MS Word, Obsidian) do not surface authorship signals inline.
- High‑fidelity export pipeline purpose‑built for essays:
  - Clio’s DOCX→HTML→PDF path (mammoth + puppeteer) preserves headings, lists, and images in a browser‑based workflow; Obsidian and Notion rely on Markdown/HTML exports and styling that can vary by theme or plugin, and MS Word’s PDF export is local but lacks integrated integrity context.
- Minimal, exam‑friendly UI:
  - Clio streamlines controls for essay writing and integrity feedback; Notion and Obsidian emphasize knowledge management and plugin extensibility; MS Word emphasizes broad document tooling that can be distracting in timed assessments.
- Privacy‑aware signals:
  - Clio uses lightweight behavioral metrics (paste events, WPM) without collecting sensitive keystroke data; mainstream editors rarely provide integrity signals, leaving such checks to post‑hoc tools.

What Clio Does Better (vs Notion, MS Word, Obsidian)
- Notion:
  - Strengths: collaborative notes, databases, Markdown‑style content, templates.
  - Gaps Clio addresses:
    - No inline authorship integrity signals during composition.
    - Export fidelity depends on Markdown/HTML and theme styling; academic formatting consistency can vary.
    - Clio’s pipeline preserves images and structure purpose‑built for essays and supports integrity‑aware writing.
- MS Word:
  - Strengths: rich WYSIWYG editor, track changes, mature formatting, local PDF export.
  - Gaps Clio addresses:
    - No real‑time integrity heuristics (paste detection/WPM anomalies) surfaced to the writer.
    - Web‑based environment with streamlined academic workflow and integrated server conversion for consistent cross‑device PDF outputs.
- Obsidian:
  - Strengths: Markdown knowledge base, graph view, plugin ecosystem.
  - Gaps Clio addresses:
    - No built‑in authorship integrity analytics; export quality varies with Markdown and plugins.
    - Clio’s rich‑text editor and export pipeline provide predictable essay formatting with integrated image handling.

Competition Research Summary
- Google Docs:
  - Strengths: real‑time collaboration, revision history, solid PDF export.
  - Gap: lacks inline integrity signals; integrity checks are post‑hoc or external.
- Overleaf (LaTeX):
  - Strengths: precise typesetting, academic templates.
  - Gap: high learning curve for casual essays; no integrity analytics during writing.
- Plagiarism/AI‑detection tools (e.g., Turnitin‑style services):
  - Strengths: post‑submission analysis against large corpora.
  - Gap: do not guide students in real‑time while writing; Clio complements these with live signals.
- Markdown‑centric tools (Bear, Typora):
  - Strengths: clean writing experiences.
  - Gap: limited integrity awareness; export fidelity depends on themes and HTML/CSS.

Why This Matters
- Institutions increasingly need proactive authorship support, not only retrospective detection.
- Clio’s combination of integrity feedback and high‑fidelity export reduces friction and encourages honest writing habits during composition.

Evaluation Recommendations (Competition)
- Usability: SUS and task time comparisons across Clio, Notion, Obsidian, MS Word on a standardized essay task.
- Fidelity: quantitative checks for heading levels, list items, image counts and sizes between authoring and exported PDF.
- Integrity signals: measure correlation of detected paste events and WPM anomalies with known ground‑truth interventions.
- Focus: subjective focus ratings with and without ambient support (Spotify integration) in time‑boxed tasks.

Risks and Mitigations
- Risk: integrity heuristics may produce false positives/negatives.
  - Mitigation: keep signals explainable, let instructors review context, avoid punitive automation.
- Risk: export fidelity variance for highly complex layouts.
  - Mitigation: expand style mappings, add academic templates, test across sample documents.
